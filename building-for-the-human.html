Insurance, in plain language
Reviewed annually

The $260K gap, made visible.

75-80% of Eaton Fire homeowners were underinsured. Average policy $628K, average rebuild $867K, lowest contractor bids $906K. The app pulls your policy, compares it against current rebuild cost per square foot in your neighborhood, and shows you what would actually happen if your home were a total loss tomorrow. Including the code-upgrade gap that like-for-like coverage doesn't cover. So you can close the gap on your terms (adjust coverage, add riders, build savings) instead of finding out the hard way.

What else lives in this phase
First, what's ALE?
Additional Living Expenses, in plain language
ALE is the part of your homeowner policy that covers the gap between your normal cost of living and what you'll spend when you can't live in your home. Hotel, rental, meals out, storage, pet boarding. It has a cap, usually a dollar amount or a time limit. Twelve months is common; some policies cap at 24. Most rebuilds run 18 to 24 months. So one of the first numbers worth knowing about your policy is when ALE expires relative to how long you'd realistically be displaced. The app surfaces both side by side, in plain language, so you can see whether they line up, or where the gap is.
Once a year
Annual rebuild-cost recalibration
Rebuild costs in fire-affected areas surged 15-20% post-Eaton from labor and materials demand. Your coverage doesn't keep pace automatically. The app re-runs the gap math each year and flags when policy increases lag the market.
FAIR Plan reality
If you're on the state insurer of last resort
FAIR Plan policies in Altadena covered 41% less than actual rebuild costs on average. If you've been non-renewed and pushed onto FAIR, the gap is structural and large. The app surfaces what supplemental coverage exists and what it would cost.
Personal property
The 2,500-row inventory
Insurance personal property claims after a total loss run to 2,500-row spreadsheets reconstructed from memory of everything you owned over the past decade. The home inventory feature exists explicitly to prevent this. One wide-angle video walkthrough now beats three months of recall later.
Flood, earthquake, sewer
Coverage exclusions worth knowing
Standard homeowner policies exclude flood, earthquake, and often sewer backup. Each is a separate rider or policy. The app flags every exclusion in your policy and tells you what it would cost to add coverage, tied to your zone's specific risk profile.
Your lender's stake in this
Mortgage clause requirements
Your mortgage requires you to carry insurance sufficient to protect the lender's collateral, usually at least the loan balance. If your coverage falls below that threshold, your lender can force-place a policy on you at higher cost. The app surfaces your loan balance against your dwelling coverage in the same view, flags when you're at risk of force-placement, and prompts a conversation with your lender's disaster line before issues compound.

The hours before everything moves.

A fire weather watch isn't an evacuation order. It's the National Weather Service saying conditions are right for fast fire spread if anything ignites. During the Eaton Fire, the gap between watch issuance and the 1:40am evacuation order was less than nine hours. The app uses the watch as the moment to start preparing. Pre-pack the bag while you're calm. Pre-book a shelter while inventory is full and pricing is the protected rate. Top off the gas before the queues start. Acting now means choice. Acting at 1:40am means whatever's left.

What else lives in this phase

Watches and warnings, not just county-wide alerts
The app pulls from the National Weather Service, your local fire department, and county emergency feeds, surfacing warnings tied to your address, not just your zip. A red flag warning two valleys over is not the same as one in your foothill. The app filters accordingly so you're not desensitized by alerts that don't apply to your block.
Pre-booking advantage
Protected pricing, before the surge
The protected pricing network honors the pre-negotiated rate from the moment a watch is issued in your zone, not just from the moment of an evacuation order. Booking on the watch means you have a confirmed bed, a confirmed price, and a confirmed pet policy 12 hours before the rest of your neighborhood wakes up to alarms.
For households with kids
Talk through it once, calmly
A watch is a chance to walk through the plan with the household when nobody is panicking. The app surfaces a short conversation guide for talking to children about evacuation, age-appropriate, with a script for "what we'll do if we have to leave." Children process pre-talked plans dramatically better than mid-disaster surprises.
Behavioral science
Why most people don't act on watches
Most people read "watch" and assume "warning will come if it's serious." That's optimism bias. Studies after major wildfires show households who acted during the watch evacuated 4-6 hours earlier on average and reported significantly lower stress. The app frames the watch as the action moment without making it feel alarmist. Calm urgency, grounded in local data.

02:11 · 24 minutes later

A real bed at a fair price, near people you trust.

Hotel apps crashed the night of the Eaton Fire. Three hotels turned Sarah away. The fourth charged $289 for one room with a broken heater. The protected pricing network is the supply-side coalition play this app requires. Partner hotels agree in advance to a single, pre-negotiated rate during declared emergencies. Every property in the network at the same price, with low or no variation, capped for the first 7 nights. No surge, no negotiation, no surprise. The community layer is what makes it human. Knowing the people you trust are heading to the same place is itself stabilizing.

What else lives in this phase

One price across the whole network, for 7 nights
Partner hotels sign on in advance to a single regional rate (with low variation for category and location), held for the first 7 nights of any declared emergency. It removes the worst-case dynamic of the night of a disaster: desperate families bidding against each other while inventory disappears. The 7-day window is the bridge. Enough time to find longer-term housing in the next phase without panic-paying for it.

Receipts and reimbursements
Week 3 of displacement

Every receipt, routed to the right form.

Displacement is the most expensive month of your life. Most of it is reimbursable through ALE, FEMA's lodging program, or as a federal casualty loss deduction. But the rules are unforgiving: itemized receipts, timestamps, merchant names, category tags, line by line. Sarah submitted her insurance claim with a fraction of what she'd actually spent because she couldn't find half the receipts when the form was due. The app captures every receipt with a phone-camera scan, auto-categorizes by destination, and pre-attaches each one to every form it belongs to: insurance ALE claim, FEMA lodging reimbursement, Q3 disaster loss tax form. When the insurer or FEMA asks, the documentation is already there.

What else lives in this phase
How auto-categorization works
Camera scan · merchant · category · destination
OCR pulls amount, merchant, date, line items. The app classifies merchant type, then applies the rules each program uses. Lodging routes to FEMA. Meals over your normal grocery baseline route to ALE. Replacement clothes and prescriptions route to casualty loss deduction. Confidence indicator on every tag, with a tap to override.
Skip the camera entirely
Optional bank and card integration
For users who'd rather not photograph every receipt, the app can connect to a credit card or bank account (read-only) and auto-import transactions during your displacement window. Same categorization, no camera required. Privacy-first: connection is opt-in, can be revoked anytime, and the app never holds login credentials directly.
The categories that actually matter
What each program will and won't reimburse
ALE covers the gap above your normal cost of living: hotels, restaurants minus what you'd normally spend on groceries, storage units, pet boarding, laundromats, replacement clothes for the season. FEMA's direct hotel program reimburses a single nightly rate. Casualty loss deductions cover anything documented and not otherwise reimbursed. The app handles the math so you don't have to learn the rules.
Insurer vs. FEMA documentation
Different rules, same scan
Insurance adjusters often want monthly summaries plus original receipts. FEMA wants timestamped photos. State tax forms want totals by category. One scan generates the format each one needs. No re-keying, no resubmitting, no scrambling at the deadline.

Air quality and contamination
What's still in the house after a fire
Pre-1975 homes have lead paint. When neighborhoods burn, particulates settle indoors and in soil for months. Two-thirds of Eaton-area soil samples exceeded EPA lead thresholds. The app tracks pre- and post-remediation testing, ties results to county health records, and flags when "back home" doesn't yet mean "safe to be home."
Code upgrade gap continues
Insurance supplements you'll have to fight for
Initial insurance estimates almost always come in low. Code-required upgrades (fire-resistant materials, updated electrical, new setbacks) get rejected as "betterment" rather than covered repairs. The supplement claim is where 30-50% of your payout actually lives. The app tracks every line item, drafts the supplement letter, and escalates when it stalls.
Lender at the table
Escrow releases at construction milestones
When insurance pays out for repairs, your lender typically holds the funds in escrow and releases them in tranches as construction milestones complete: foundation, framing, electrical, final. Each release requires a contractor invoice, an inspection sign-off, and a request to the lender's loss-draft department. The app tracks every milestone, flags when a release is overdue, and pre-fills the lender request so a missed payment doesn't stall the contractor.
Disaster loss tax
The deduction nobody tells you about
Federally declared disasters unlock specific casualty loss deduction categories. Most disaster survivors don't know about it. The app tracks what's deductible, what documentation your accountant needs, and whether to claim it on the current year or amend the prior year (sometimes worth thousands more, depending on income).
For the kids
Pediatric mental health post-disaster
Children process disaster differently at different ages. Returning home can re-trigger anxiety they were managing while displaced. The app surfaces specialists in disaster-related child therapy who accept your insurance, and offers age-appropriate resources for parents on what's normal at month 6 vs. what warrants a referral.
Financial runway
Month 4 · Recovery

The cliff, visible in month four.

ALE coverage has a hard expiration that most policies cap at 12 months. Rebuilds routinely run 18 to 24. Sarah's policy and her timeline were on a collision course she couldn't see. The app surfaces the cliff in month four, not month eleven, and shows the full runway alongside it: ALE remaining, FEMA grants pending, personal reserves, available credit, all in one number. Then it surfaces gap-closing options in order of cost: grants first, low-interest disaster loans, credit last. So you reach for the right option first, not the most expensive one in a panic.

What else lives in this phase
Coverage stretch
ALE extensions and hardship programs
When ALE is on a collision course with rebuild timeline, options exist before the cliff: insurer-side extensions for documented delays, FEMA hardship programs, state disaster funds. Most of these require asking before you've run out. The app prompts at the right time.
Your lender as runway
Mortgage forbearance most people don't ask for
Federally declared disasters trigger lender obligations under FHA, Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and VA rules: forbearance for 3 to 12 months, deferred payments, and no negative credit reporting for affected borrowers. Most homeowners don't know to ask, and lenders rarely call to offer. The app surfaces what your specific lender's disaster program looks like, drafts the forbearance request on your behalf, and tracks the timeline so payments resume cleanly when you're ready.
Where is my money
Across every source, in real time
Applied for, approved, received, outstanding. Across FEMA, SBA, your insurer, state programs, community grants, GoFundMe. One ledger, one truth. Sarah was reconstructing this in a notebook for fourteen months.

If underinsured
Gap financing options
SBA disaster loans, CDFI programs, community foundations, faith-based assistance, all surfaced in order of cost and friction. For the 75-80% of Eaton Fire homeowners who were underinsured, this is the difference between rebuilding and not.
Your block · Rosita Drive
Month 8 · Recovery

Cheaper, faster, less lonely. Together.

Twelve households on the same block negotiating with the same contractor have buying power no individual homeowner has. Group rates run 15-20% under solo bids. Coordinated permit submissions move faster through county queues. Shared legal counsel and public adjusters split costs five or six ways. The app makes this kind of collective action possible without anyone having to organize it manually. It surfaces active group offers, shows what worked in past fire-recovery zones, and uses the rebuild map as a quiet motivator: progress is contagious.

Checking in
Month 8 · the long middle

The hardest months are often quieter than the first one.

Months 3 to 6 of recovery are typically harder than the acute phase. The adrenaline is gone. The path is still unclear. The kids are processing something they don't have language for. And nobody quite knows what to do for you. Not your insurer, not FEMA, not the county, not even the closest people who haven't been through it. The app names the plateau out loud and surfaces what helps: a brief weekly check-in, a trend line so you can see where you actually are, a short list of disaster-specialty therapists who take your insurance, and voices from survivors at month 8 and month 14 who can describe what's coming next.

Voices, not testimonials

Routine as anchor
Keeping one thing that survived
Disaster recovery research consistently finds that survivors who maintain at least one pre-disaster routine (a regular meal, a Sunday tradition, a weekly call to a parent) recover faster and report lower long-term distress. The app helps identify and protect that anchor when the rest of the schedule is in flux.

Before you close
Month 14 · Returning home

What you'll want to know before you close.

Rebuilding doesn't reset the underlying financial risk of your property. The market has already repriced your neighborhood. Insurance carriers, lenders, and secondary-market investors all see your street differently than they did before. The app surfaces these facts in plain language so they're never a surprise when you refinance, sell, or renew coverage. And it connects you to the policy and advocacy work pushing for change, in case you want to channel what you've learned into something durable.
